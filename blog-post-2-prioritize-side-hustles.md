## Full Article

You have four hours free this Saturday. Maybe five if you skip the grocery run.

You could work on:
- The Etsy shop that made $347 last month
- The YouTube channel you've been "meaning to be consistent with"
- The freelance client project that's due next week
- The online course you're 60% done building
- The newsletter you promised yourself you'd send weekly

So which one gets your time?

If you're like most multi-project entrepreneurs, you pick whichever one feels most urgent, most exciting, or least scary in that particular moment. Sometimes you spend 30 minutes deciding, then another hour switching between projects, and suddenly your Saturday's gone and you've made zero meaningful progress on anything.

Welcome to the opportunity cost trap—where having multiple possibilities somehow leaves you with nothing to show for it.

Let me show you a better way.

## The Data: You're Not Alone (But Most People Are Failing)

Before we get to the solution, let's talk about what we're really dealing with.

**Current state of side hustlers:**

- 45% of Americans have a side hustle (Bankrate, 2024)
- The average side hustler manages 2.3 different projects simultaneously

**But here's what's interesting:**

The most successful multi-project entrepreneurs (those earning over $50K/year from side hustles) have one thing in common: **they don't work on everything equally.** They ruthlessly prioritize based on current metrics and potential, and they shift focus frequently based on what's working.

They treat their portfolio of projects like a venture capitalist treats investments:
- Place multiple bets
- Monitor performance constantly
- Double down on winners
- Cut losses quickly
- Rebalance regularly

The difference between struggling and thriving isn't having fewer projects—it's having a better decision-making system.

> "You can do anything, but not everything—especially not everything at once." — David Allen (Getting Things Done)

## The Real Problem: Everything Feels Equally Important

Here's why choosing is so hard:

**The YouTube channel** might not make money yet, but what if you're one viral video away from 100K subscribers?

**The Etsy shop** is already profitable, but growth has plateaued. Should you optimize what's working or explore new opportunities?

**The freelance work** pays the bills, but it's trading time for money. Shouldn't you focus on more scalable ventures?

**The online course** could generate passive income forever, but it's been "almost done" for three months. Is it time to just finish the damn thing?

Each project has a legitimate case for your attention. Each has potential. Each would benefit from more focus.

And that's exactly the problem.

Without a systematic way to evaluate them, you're making decisions based on:
- Whichever one feels most fun today (novelty bias)
- Whichever one feels most urgent (reactivity bias)
- Whichever one you're most scared of neglecting (guilt-driven productivity)
- Whichever one someone just asked you about (social pressure)

These aren't strategies. They're just different flavors of decision paralysis.

## The Weekly Focus Framework

Here's the system I developed after watching dozens of successful multi-project entrepreneurs (and struggling through years of my own chaotic switching):

Instead of trying to work on everything all the time, you **assign one primary focus project per week.**

This doesn't mean you abandon everything else—it means that when you have discretionary time, you know exactly where it goes. No decision fatigue. No guilt. No switching costs.

### How It Works (The 4-Step Process)

**Step 1: Weekly Review (Sunday or Monday, 20 minutes)**

List all your active projects and gather current metrics:

- Revenue (last 30 days)
- Growth trajectory (up, flat, or down)
- Time invested (hours per week)
- ROI (rough $/hour)
- Completion status (if it's a buildable thing)
- External dependencies (waiting on others?)

Don't overthink this. You're not building a financial model—you're just getting oriented.

**Step 2: Score Each Project (5 minutes)**

Rate each venture on these criteria (1-5 scale):

| Criteria | What You're Evaluating |
|----------|------------------------|
| **Momentum** | Is this gaining traction? New customers, growing audience, increasing engagement? |
| **Revenue** | Making money now or very close to it? |
| **Leverage** | Will effort this week compound? (Building assets, automating, creating content libraries) |
| **Urgency** | Real deadline or time-sensitive opportunity? |
| **Energy** | Am I excited to work on this, or dreading it? |

**Total possible points: 25**

**Example scoring:**

```
Project: YouTube Channel
- Momentum: 4 (last video got 3x normal views)
- Revenue: 2 (monetized but minimal income)
- Leverage: 5 (content libraries + algorithm learning)
- Urgency: 2 (no deadlines)
- Energy: 5 (really excited about next video idea)
TOTAL: 18/25

Project: Etsy Shop
- Momentum: 2 (sales flat for 8 weeks)
- Revenue: 4 (consistent $300-400/month)
- Leverage: 2 (time-for-money, manual fulfillment)
- Urgency: 1 (no pressing issues)
- Energy: 2 (feels stale, not excited)
TOTAL: 11/25
```

**Step 3: Assign Your Weekly Focus**

The highest score gets 70% of your available side-hustle time this week.

The next 1-2 get "maintenance mode" (20% total)—just enough to keep them alive:
- Answer customer emails
- Fulfill orders
- Post something minimal
- Handle critical tasks only

Everything else gets parked. Completely. They'll get their turn when the metrics shift.

**Step 4: Define Your Weekly Win (5 minutes)**

For your primary focus project, decide on *one* clear outcome that would make this week successful.

Not a list of tasks. One outcome.

**Bad weekly win:** "Work on YouTube channel"
**Good weekly win:** "Publish one video and write scripts for the next two"

**Bad weekly win:** "Improve Etsy shop"
**Good weekly win:** "Test 5 new product titles and photograph 3 new items"

This gives you clarity and a finish line. You'll know by Sunday whether you won the week.

## The Scoring System Deep Dive

Let's break down what each criterion is really measuring:

### Momentum (1-5): Is the market responding?

- **5 points:** Clear growth signals—new customers, increasing traffic, audience engagement rising
- **3 points:** Flat but stable—consistent performance, no growth but no decline
- **1 point:** Declining—fewer visitors, dropping engagement, customers leaving

**Why it matters:** Momentum is the market telling you something is working. When you have it, pour fuel on the fire. Most people miss this by spreading attention equally across all projects regardless of performance.

### Revenue (1-5): Show me the money

- **5 points:** Generating $500+/month consistently
- **3 points:** Making some money ($50-500/month) or has had recent sales
- **1 point:** No revenue yet, but validated demand (emails collected, pre-orders, etc.)

**Why it matters:** Revenue is reality. It's the difference between a hobby and a business. Projects making money deserve different treatment than projects you *hope* will make money.

### Leverage (1-5): Does this compound?

- **5 points:** High leverage—content libraries, automation, hiring, building assets that work without you
- **3 points:** Medium leverage—improving systems, building skills, creating reusable templates
- **1 point:** Low leverage—time-for-money work that doesn't build equity

**Why it matters:** Not all hours are equal. An hour spent building a course or creating evergreen content pays dividends forever. An hour spent fulfilling one-off custom orders disappears.

### Urgency (1-5): Is there a real deadline?

- **5 points:** Hard external deadline (client due date, event, seasonal opportunity closing)
- **3 points:** Soft deadline (you set it, but it matters)
- **1 point:** No deadline—could do it anytime

**Why it matters:** Some things genuinely can't wait. But be honest—most of our "urgent" feelings are self-imposed. Real urgency should be rare. If everything scores 5 here, you've over-committed.

### Energy (1-5): Do you actually want to do this?

- **5 points:** Genuinely excited, ideas flowing, can't wait to work on it
- **3 points:** Neutral—it's fine, not dreading it but not pumped
- **1 point:** Dragging your feet, procrastinating, feels like a slog

**Why it matters:** You're doing this outside your day job. If you're forcing yourself through something you hate, you'll burn out or quit. Energy is a resource, not a moral failing. Sometimes the best move is to pause low-energy projects until inspiration returns.

## Common Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

### Mistake #1: Chasing Shiny Objects

**What it looks like:** Your primary focus keeps changing every week because you had a new idea or saw someone else's success.

**The fix:** Lock in your weekly focus on Sunday and don't revisit it until the following Sunday. New ideas are great—capture them, but don't let them hijack the current week.

### Mistake #2: Ignoring Energy Scores

**What it looks like:** You keep prioritizing the project with highest revenue even though you're completely burned out on it.

**The fix:** If a project consistently scores 1-2 on Energy for 3+ weeks in a row, something needs to change. Either take a complete break, delegate part of it, or honestly assess whether you should wind it down.

### Mistake #3: Never Letting Anything Die

**What it looks like:** You have 6 "active" projects but only 10 hours/week to work on side hustles. Everything gets 1.5 hours and nothing moves forward.

**The fix:** If a project scores below 12 points for 4 consecutive weeks and you have more than 3 active projects, it's time to either pause it officially or kill it. Archive the work, document lessons learned, and free up mental space.

### Mistake #4: Optimizing for Revenue Only

**What it looks like:** Always choosing the highest-paying project, even when something else has better momentum or leverage.

**The fix:** Revenue is one input, not the only input. A project making $50/month with high momentum and leverage might deserve focus over a $500/month project that's pure time-for-money with no growth potential.

### Mistake #5: Forgetting to Rescore Weekly

**What it looks like:** You decided in January that Project X was your focus and now it's April and you're still grinding on it even though the metrics changed months ago.

**The fix:** This only works if you actually do the weekly review. Put it on your calendar. 20 minutes every Sunday or Monday morning. No negotiating.
